How to Rent an Apartment in Japan as a Foreigner: A Step-by-Step Guide

Updated April 14, 2026

Imagine landing in Tokyo after a 14-hour flight, your suitcase stuffed with everything you own

The last thing you need is to stand in a real estate office, nodding along to terms like key money and guarantor company while your jet lag clouds your understanding. That’s how I felt in 2018, standing in a cramped Shinjuku office with a 10-page contract in front of me. The agent spoke in rapid Japanese, and the only word I caught was shikikin. Three months later, I’d paid ¥150,000 in non-refundable fees for a studio so small my suitcase wouldn’t open all the way.

The first step is always the hardest

Walk into any Tokyo real estate site like SRE House or At Home, and you’ll see listings labeled 外国人向け—foreign-friendly. But friendly doesn’t mean transparent. The real hurdle isn’t finding a place; it’s understanding what you’re agreeing to when you sign.

First, decide where you want to live. If you’re working in central Tokyo, a shinjuku-ku studio might save you two hours of commuting, but your shikikin (deposit) could double because of high demand. In Saitama or Chiba, you’ll get more space, but the commute might wear you down over time.

Then there’s the layout. A 1K is a studio with a separate kitchen area. A 1DK has a dining room. The difference between the two is often just a 3-meter partition wall, but it can feel like night and day when you’re living inside it.

You’ll need more than just your passport

Japanese landlords are risk-averse. Without a Japanese guarantor, you’ll need a guarantor company. The most common are Global Trust Networks and ARC Global. They charge a fee equivalent to one month’s rent for their service, plus a yearly renewal fee of around ¥10,000. Some agencies bundle this into the contract; others make you pay upfront. Always ask for the breakdown.

Then there’s the reikin—often called “key money” in English. It’s a gift to the landlord, usually one to two months’ rent, and it’s non-refundable. I once paid ¥50,000 in key money for a 10-square-meter studio in Shinjuku. Three years later, I moved out, and the landlord didn’t even acknowledge the payment beyond a quick nod when I handed over the key.

Common mistake number one: confusing shikikin (deposit) with reikin (key money). The deposit is refundable, minus cleaning fees. The key money? Gone forever. Another mistake is assuming your agency will explain the difference. They won’t. You have to ask.

Money talks, but it doesn’t always tell the truth

Your budget isn’t just the monthly rent. You’ll need:

  • Key money: 1–2 months’ rent
  • Deposit (shikikin): 1–2 months’ rent
  • Guarantor fee: 1 month’s rent
  • Agent fee: 1 month’s rent
  • Fire insurance: ¥15,000–¥25,000
  • Initial utility setup: ¥10,000–¥20,000

That’s five to seven months’ rent upfront. If your rent is ¥80,000, expect to pay ¥400,000–¥560,000 before you move in. The morning you sign the contract, the agent will hand you a stack of forms and a pen. The numbers blur together. You’ll sign anyway. Everyone does.

Pro tip: Some agencies offer “zero key money” deals, but they often come with higher monthly rent or hidden fees. Always calculate the total cost over two years, not just the upfront payment. A place with ¥50,000 key money and ¥85,000 rent might cost you more in the long run than a ¥100,000 rent with no key money.

Contract day is when the real work begins

The contract will be in Japanese. If you don’t read Japanese, bring a translator or use a service like Japan Translation Services. Don’t rely on your agency’s summary—they’ll skip the fine print about breaking the lease or subletting.

Look for these clauses:

  • 解約予告期間 (notice period): Usually 1–2 months
  • 違約金 (penalty fee): Often one month’s rent if you break the lease early
  • 更新料 (renewal fee): Typically ¥10,000–¥50,000 every two years

Common mistake number two: assuming you can sublet if you move out early. Most contracts forbid it. If you need flexibility, look for フリーレント (free rent) apartments or short-term leases through companies like Saboten House.

The agent will ask for your inkan—a personal seal you’ll press into each form. If you don’t have one, buy a simple 実印 (registered seal) at a stationery store like Loft. It costs ¥2,000–¥5,000. Don’t use a pencil or a signature; landlords take the inkan seriously.

The apartment won’t feel like home for a long time

Even after you move in, the space won’t feel yours. The walls are thin. The neighbors will hear you walking. The morning light spills over your balcony’s railing, but the floor is cold. You’ll step on a creaky floorboard and wonder if it’s broken.

For the first month, everything feels temporary. The futon on the floor is fine. The single-burner stove works. But the fridge hums all night, and the washing machine is in the hallway, shared with three other units. You’ll start noticing the flaws—the chipped sink, the drafty window, the way the shower curtain never quite dries.

It takes about three months for a place to stop feeling like a temporary solution. By then, you’ll have memorized the rhythm of the building: the 7 AM elevator that smells like coffee, the neighbor who practices piano at 9 PM, the way the mailbox clatters when the postman drops the day’s flyers inside.

When it’s time to move out, don’t expect a clean slate

You’ll need to schedule an inspection with the landlord or agency. They’ll check for damage beyond normal wear and tear. Scuffs on the floor? That’s a ¥10,000 deduction. A stain on the tatami? ¥5,000. Even a tiny hole in the wall from a nail will cost you ¥3,000–¥5,000 to fix.

Common mistake: assuming your deposit will cover the cleaning fee. Most agencies charge ¥30,000–¥50,000 for professional cleaning, and they’ll deduct that from your deposit before returning the rest. If the deposit isn’t enough, you’ll owe the difference. Always take photos on move-in day and keep them for the move-out inspection.

On the last day, you’ll return the key, the remote controls, even the tiny trash can in the kitchenette. The landlord will nod, hand you a receipt, and that’s it. No ceremony. No fanfare. Just another exchange in a system designed to keep things moving smoothly for everyone but you.

But it gets easier

After my first apartment, I moved four more times in Japan. Each time, the process felt less like stepping into the unknown and more like navigating a well-worn path. I learned to ask for English contracts. I started negotiating key money. I even convinced a landlord to waive the renewal fee by offering to renew early.

Now, when I walk into a real estate office, the jargon doesn’t feel like a barrier. The contracts don’t feel like traps. The key money and deposits are just part of the rhythm—something to account for, like the cost of groceries or train fare. The space still isn’t perfect. The walls are thin. The neighbors are loud. But the futon is mine. The morning light is predictable. And for the first time in years, the floor feels like a place to stand, not just a place to leave.
